UNIT 1

Propositions ,Logical Operations (basic connectives),Compound Statements,Construction of Truth Table,Quantifiers,Conditional Statements,Tautology,Contradiction,Contingency,Logical Equivalence,CNF & DNF,Rules of Inference,Modus Ponens,Modus Tollens,Syllogism,Proof by Contradiction,Mathematical Induction.

Quick Revision:

1. Propositions A proposition is a declarative statement that is either True (T) or False (F) but not both. * Example: 2+2=4 (T) Questions and commands are not propositions.

2. Logical Operations (Basic Connectives) ¬p → Negation (Not p) p ∧ q → Conjunction (AND) p V q → Disjunction (OR) p → q → Implication (If p then q) p ↔ q → Biconditional (If and only if) * Key Point: AND → True only when both True OR → False only when both False

3. Compound Statements Formed by combining simple propositions using logical connectives. *Example: (p ∧ q), (p → q), (¬p V q)

4. Construction of Truth Table Steps: 1. Count number of variables (n).
2. Total rows = 2ⁿ.
3. Fill combinations of T and F.
4. Apply logical operations step by step.
* Important: Always solve inside brackets first.

5. Quantifiers Used in predicate logic.
∀ → For all
∃ → There exists
Example: ∀x (x² ≥ 0) → True ∃x (x² = 4) → True
Negation Rules: ¬(∀x P(x)) = ∃x ¬P(x) ¬(∃x P(x)) = ∀x ¬P(x)

6. Conditional Statements p → q (If p then q)
Only False when: p = True and q = False
Related Forms: . Converse → q → p . Inverse → ¬p → ¬q . Contrapositive → ¬q → ¬p (Logically Equivalent to original)

7. Tautology A statement always True.
* Example: p V ¬p

8. Contradiction A statement that is always False.
* Example: p ∧ ¬p

9. Contingency Sometimes True, Sometimes False. Most logical statements fall under this.

10. Logical Equivalence Two statements are logically equivalent if their truth tables are identical.
Notation: p ≡ q
Important Laws: Identity Law , Domination Law , Idempotent Law , Double Negation , De Morgan's Laws , Implication Law
* Example: p → q ≡ ¬p V q

11. CNF & DNF DNF (Disjunctive Normal Form): OR of AND terms
CNF (Conjunctive Normal Form): AND of OR terms Used in digital logic and simplification.

12. Rules of Inference Used to derive conclusions logically.
Common Rules: Modus Ponens , Modus Tollens , Hypothetical Syllogism , Disjunctive Syllogism

13. Proof by Contradiction 1. Assume opposite of what you want to prove.
2. Derive contradiction.
3. Therefore original statement is true.

14. Mathematical Induction Used to prove statements for natural numbers.
Steps: 1. Base Case: Prove for n = 1 (or starting value).
2. Inductive Hypothesis: Assume true for n = k.
3. Inductive Step: Prove true for n = k+1.
4. Conclusion: True for all n ∈ N.
